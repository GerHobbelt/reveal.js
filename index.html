Why Is Debuggin Important?

More of your professional career will be spent debuggin rather than codin!

As soon as we started programming, we found to our surprise that it wasn't as easy to get programs right as we had thought. Debugging had to be discovered. I can remember the exact instant when I realized that a large part of my life from then on was going to be spent in finding mistakes in my own programs.
-- Maurice Wilkes discovers bugs, 1949

To understand why Debuggin is important, you must know:

  • What Debuggin is
  • What Bugs are
  • What the difference between Codin and Debuggin is.

What is Debuggin?

Debuggin is developin an understandin of a codin error, so that the behavior of the code and your expectations of it match.


  • What is in your mind isn't always reality.
  • What is in the code sometimes does more than you observe.

What are Bugs?

Bugs are a Programmer's Errors!

(Sometimes your errors...)


This can be:

  • Error understanding the initial problem.
  • Error in assumption of the behavior of the code.
  • Error caused by environment and mental influence.

A bug is a mismatch between your mental model of what the code should do and what the code actually does.

What is the Difference Between Codin and Debuggin?

Debuggin is comin to terms with reality.

Codin is attemptin to make what is in your mind a reality.


With careful thought and great care, it is possible to never have to debug your own code. Not all are so lucky

Gonna splain a series of actions you can take to make you a better debugger.

The key to GREAT debuggin is a to have a method or process.

Gather Information

Information comes from many sources. Here are a few you need to look at when debuggin.

  • Logs: Knowin the location of all your server's logs is important. Even more important is knowing what kind of imformation you can find in them.
  • Bug Reports: Teach your users to make good bug reports this way you get more information with doing less exploration.
  • Pictures: Sometimes it's true that a picture is worth a 1000 words.

More information is almost always better.

Replication

Without Replication, there is no real way to know if a bug has been fixed.

Replication is also just another way to gather information.

Use Replication to narrow down the variables.


Things to keep in mind when replicating:

  • Can you replicate it on a copy of the site?
  • How does the user that reported this use the site?
  • Do you have enough information to accuratly replicate the issue?

Path to Recovery

After Replication, How do we recover?


Follow a process:

  • Cheap Tests First: Be lazy! Try:
    • Clear all caches (browser, Drupal, Varnish, any other)
    • Check your branch Make sure you have the right code.
    • Check for old js/css Use your browser to see if the CDN or caching is happening.
    • Keep a checklist of Dumb Mistakes Learn from your mistakes. Keep in mind the symptoms.

Path to Recovery


  • Did Anyone Else Solve It?
    • Hit up the Drupal.org issue queues.
    • Search for exact error messages minus varibles.
    • Ask on #drupal-support.
  • Get Dirty!
    • Change One Thing at a Time.
    • Quit Think about how it works and Look at how it works.
    • Keep an audit trail. (Comments in code, ticket or some other trackable medium)
    • Be skeptical of Magic fixes.

In Conclusion

  • Think! Don't blindly use checklists.
